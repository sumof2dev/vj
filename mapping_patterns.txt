Executive Summary
The contemporary landscape of entertainment lighting has been fundamentally altered by the
democratization of laser projection technology. Once the exclusive domain of high-budget
touring productions and specialized laser companies, coherent light fixtures have permeated the
enthusiast, semi-professional, and mobile DJ markets. This shift is largely driven by Original
Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs) based in the Shenzhen and Guangzhou manufacturing hubs,
producing hardware marketed under a myriad of brands including Ehaho, Shehds, Big Dipper,
U'King, Sumger, and Chinly. While the optical hardware—diode arrays and
galvanometers—has become commoditized and surprisingly robust, the control infrastructure
remains a complex, often obfuscated labyrinth of non-standardized protocols and proprietary
firmware logic.
This report provides a definitive, expert-level technical analysis of the DMX512 control
implementation within these generic laser systems. Moving beyond the rudimentary 12-channel
profiles that serve as a functional baseline, this research deconstructs the highly compact
6-channel modes and the sophisticated, professional-grade 34-channel dual-layer engines
found in modern fixtures like the Ehaho L2600 and Shehds Constellation series. Through a
rigorous synthesis of user manuals, firmware behaviors, and cross-brand architectural
comparisons, the analysis validates the hypothesis that a unified "ShowNET-derived" standard
governs the high-channel behaviors of these diverse brands. Furthermore, the report explores
the critical implications of these control maps on operational safety, galvanometer longevity, and
artistic workflow, providing systems integrators with the requisite knowledge to deploy these
cost-effective units in professional environments safely and effectively.

1. The Architectural Landscape of Generic Laser
Control
To comprehend the nuances of DMX mappings—specifically the divergent philosophies behind
6-channel and 34-channel modes—one must first analyze the internal architecture of the laser
projector itself. Unlike conventional intelligent lighting fixtures, which manipulate the intensity or
position of an incoherent light source, a laser projector is a vector-based graphic device. The
DMX signal does not control the beam directly; rather, it instructs a microcontroller to execute
complex mathematical macros that drive the X/Y galvanometers (galvos) and modulate the RGB

laser diodes.

1.1 The Shift from ILDA to DMX-First Architectures
Historically, the control of entertainment lasers was bifurcated: simple "sound-to-light" fixtures
for discos and ILDA-controlled projectors for professionals. The ILDA (International Laser
Display Association) standard relies on a DB-25 parallel connection carrying raw analog voltage
signals to the scanners and color lines. However, the generic market has aggressively pivoted
toward a "DMX-First" architecture.
In this paradigm, the mainboard acts as an embedded computer. It stores a library of
pre-calculated vector paths (patterns) on an internal ROM or SD card. When a user sends a
DMX value of "10" on the Pattern channel, the microcontroller retrieves the corresponding
vector file, applies geometric transformations (calculated in real-time), and outputs the analog
signals to the galvos. Brands like Shehds and Ehaho have integrated advanced processors
capable of handling not just simple playback, but dual-layer synthesis and real-time geometric
correction, features previously exclusive to PC-based software.

1.2 The Controller Ecosystem: Microcontrollers and Latency
The performance of a generic laser is inextricably linked to the processing power of its
mainboard. Analysis of the Sumger and Chinly product lines reveals a reliance on
cost-effective ARM-based microcontrollers. While sufficient for 6-channel operation, these
processors often struggle with the computational overhead of the 34-channel mode.
When operating in 34-channel mode, the processor must calculate two independent pattern
layers (Pattern A and Pattern B), apply distinct size, rotation, and clipping modifiers to each,
composit them into a single point list, and optimize the scanner path to prevent galvo
overheating. This computational load creates a phenomenon known as "DMX Latency" or
"Frame Buffering." Unlike a moving head light, which responds instantly to a Pan/Tilt command,
a generic laser in high-channel mode may exhibit a latency of 2-5 frames (roughly 30-80ms) as
the processor recalculates the vector path. This latency is a critical consideration for lighting
designers attempting to time-code laser hits to fast-paced music; the 34-channel mode offers
more features but may feel "sluggish" compared to the leaner 6-channel mode.

1.3 The "Generic" Misnomer: OEM Lineage
While the market is flooded with dozens of brand names, the underlying control logic usually
traces back to a few key chipset providers.
●​ The "Big Dipper" Architecture: Found in older U'King models and Big Dipper branded
units (B102RGB, K800). Characterized by a "Mode" channel that strictly segments DMX,
Sound, and Auto functions into value ranges.
●​ The "ShowNET" Architecture: The dominant standard for modern 34-channel fixtures
from Ehaho, Shehds, and Ibiza Light. This protocol is derived from the Laserworld
ShowNET mainboard, allowing for highly standardized "Professional Mode" profiles.
●​ The "Pulse/Chinly" Decoder: A source of significant confusion. Chinly manufactures
both DMX LED decoders (PWM based) and Laser Projectors. A 12-channel profile for a
Chinly LED decoder will essentially disable a Chinly laser projector, as the mapping of
"Red Intensity" (CH1 on decoder) conflicts with "Mode Select" (CH1 on projector).

2. Deconstructing the 6-Channel Protocol
The 6-channel mode serves as the industry's "Lowest Common Denominator." It is designed for
maximum compatibility with basic DMX controllers, such as the ubiquitous 192-channel "DMX
Operator" style boards. In this mode, the fixture effectively behaves as a macro player, stripping
away granular control in favor of ease of use. However, distinct variations exist between the
implementation by brands like U'King versus Big Dipper.

In this mode, the laser's color is typically hard-coded to the pattern (e.g., Pattern 1 is always

3. The 34-Channel Professional Protocol: The
ShowNET Standard
The introduction of the 34-channel mode in generically branded fixtures like the Ehaho L2600,
Shehds Constellation, and Ibiza Light Scan series marks a pivotal moment in budget laser
control. Analysis confirms that this mapping is a near-direct clone of the Laserworld ShowNET
Professional Mode. This standardization allows for a level of cross-brand compatibility
previously unseen in the generic market.
The 34-channel protocol is architected around a Dual-Layer Engine, enabling the projector to
emit two distinct patterns simultaneously (Pattern A and Pattern B) and manipulate them
independently before compositing them into a single output.

3.1 Architecture of the Dual-Layer Engine
●​ Layer A (Primary): Controlled by Channels 1 through 17.
●​ Layer B (Secondary): Controlled by Channels 18 through 34.
This architecture implies that the microcontroller is interleaving the vector data of two separate
files. For example, it might draw the points of Pattern A, blank the beam, move to Pattern B,
draw Pattern B, and repeat. This doubles the mechanical demand on the galvanometers. If
Pattern A is a complex text string and Pattern B is a dense graphic, the scan rate (kpps)
effectively halves for each pattern, leading to significant flicker. Successful use of the
34-channel mode requires the operator to balance the complexity of the two layers.

The mapping for Layer B mirrors Layer A almost exactly, shifted by 17 channels.
●​ CH 18: Mode/Intensity for Layer B.
●​ CH 19: Pattern Select for Layer B.
●​ CH 24: Zoom for Layer B.
●​ ...
●​ CH 34: Reset / Setup Write.

The "Doomsday" Fader (Channel 34): On ShowNET-based systems, the highest channel
often serves a dual function. At specific values (e.g., 200-255), it triggers a system reset.
However, when combined with specific values on other channels (like CH 1 or CH 2), it can act
as a "Write to Memory" function.
●​ Risk: If a user accidentally fans their faders and pushes CH 34 to 255 while CH 4
(X-Position) is offset, the laser might permanently save that offset as the new "Center" in
its firmware.
●​ Mitigation: Lighting programmers must Park or Disable Channel 34 in their console
patch to prevent accidental overwriting of firmware defaults during live busking.

4. Brand-Specific Technical Variations
While the protocols broadly align with the standards above, OEM firmware customization
introduces specific quirks that can break standard profiles.

4.1 Ehaho (L2600 & 3D Animation Series)
Ehaho positions itself in the "semi-pro" tier. Their implementation of the 34-channel mode is
generally faithful to the ShowNET standard.
●​ Sound Sensitivity: A critical hardware limitation is that the "Sound Sensitivity" is often
controlled by a physical potentiometer on the rear chassis, not a DMX channel. Even in
DMX mode, if "Sound Active" is triggered via Channel 1, the sensitivity is dictated by that
physical knob. This prevents remote adjustment of audio triggers.
●​ Blackout Behavior: In 6-channel mode, Ehaho units often require Channel 1 to be held
at a specific high value (150-255) to emit light. Values 0-9 usually engage a physical
shutter blackout (if equipped) or diode suppression.

4.2 Shehds (Constellation Series)
Shehds integrates a modern mainboard that supports Bluetooth APP control alongside DMX.
●​ RJ45 Connectivity: Many Shehds lasers feature RJ45 ports labelled "DMX". Users often
mistake these for "ILDA over CAT5." In reality, they are usually carrying standard DMX512
signals using a proprietary pinout (often Pins 1/2 as Data+/-, ignoring standard ESTA
pinouts). Adapters are required.
●​ 19-Channel "DJ Mode": Shehds units often default to a 19-channel mode out of the box.
This is a truncated version of the 34-channel mode that exposes only Layer A and global
settings, removing the Layer B (Dual Pattern) functionality to save DMX addresses.
●​ Mapping Discrepancy: Documentation for Shehds often lists "Effect 1 (X-axis flip)" on
CH 30. In the standard ShowNET map, flip functions are often handled within the "Zoom"
channels (inverting values 128-255). This suggests Shehds uses a slightly modified
firmware branch.

4.3 Big Dipper (B102RGB)
The B102RGB is a 4-aperture laser bar (Red, Green, Blue, Purple typically). Its 15-channel
DMX mode is a "Multi-Head" implementation.
●​ CH 1: Global Mode (>192 for DMX).

●​ CH 2: Global Pattern.
●​ CH 4, 7, 10, 13: These control the "ON/OFF/STROBE" for heads 1, 2, 3, and 4
respectively.
●​ Insight: A standard single-head generic laser profile will fail here. The user must treat this
fixture as a "Pixel Bar" in their console, creating a profile that accounts for the 4 distinct
control instances for the diodes while sharing the global pattern channel.

4.4 Sumger (DM-RGB400)
The Sumger DM-RGB400 is notorious for its sparse documentation.
●​ 12-Channel Mode: This is the standard for Sumger.
○​ CH 1: Mode (0-63 Off, 64-127 Sound, 128-191 Auto, 192-255 DMX).
○​ CH 2: Pattern Group.
○​ CH 3: Pattern Selection.
○​ CH 4: Color Effect (often a macro channel cycling colors).
●​ Color Issue: Unlike the 34ch mode, the Sumger 12ch mode rarely offers individual R/G/B
fader control (0-255 mixing). Instead, CH 4 acts as a "Color Wheel" selecting preset
combinations (Red, Green, Yellow, etc.). This limits color mixing capabilities significantly.

5. Advanced Profile Strategy & Software Integration
To effectively control these generic fixtures, lighting designers must adopt specific strategies for
profile creation in professional software environments.

5.1 QLC+ (Quantum Lighting Control) Strategies
For QLC+ users, the challenge lies in the "Heads" definition.
●​ 34-Channel Profile: Create a fixture definition with 2 Heads.
○​ Map Channels 1-17 to Head 1.
○​ Map Channels 18-34 to Head 2.
○​ Define CH 1 and CH 18 as "Intensity" capability.
○​ Define CH 2 and CH 19 as "Gobo" capability (Pattern).
○​ Define CH 4/5 and CH 21/22 as "Pan/Tilt" capabilities.
●​ Benefit: This setup allows QLC+ to use its RGB Matrix functions on the laser. You can
run a "Plasma" effect across Head 1 and Head 2, causing the two laser layers to
modulate colors independently.

5.2 GrandMA2 / MA3 Strategies
●​ Fixture Type: The "Laserworld DS-1000RGB" 34-channel profile is the "Golden Master"
for Shehds/Ehaho units. It is widely available on the MA Sharenet.
●​ XYZ Mapping: While generic lasers are not 3D objects, mapping the X-Position (CH 4)
and Y-Position (CH 5) to the Pan and Tilt attributes in the MA profile allows the use of the
trackball and "Follow" modes in the 3D visualizer.
●​ Gobo Bitmap: Since generic lasers use standard ILDA frames, users can import a
generic "ILDA Gobo Bitmap" set into MA2 to visualize the patterns (Circle, Square,
Tunnel) on the console layout views, rather than seeing "Gobo 1," "Gobo 2."

5.3 SoundSwitch Strategy
SoundSwitch is increasingly popular for controlling generic lasers alongside DJ hardware.
●​ The "Generic" Fallback: SoundSwitch includes a "Generic DMX Laser" profile. However,
this profile usually assumes a 9-channel map.
●​ Custom Map for 34ch: Users must create a custom fixture in the SoundSwitch fixture
manager.
○​ Map "Color" to CH 9 (Color Select).
○​ Map "Gobo" to CH 2 (Pattern).
○​ Crucial: Map "Strobe" to CH 1 (Intensity). In SoundSwitch, the "Strobe" attribute
often handles the ON/OFF switching. If mapped incorrectly, the laser will not
blackout during breaks.

6. Operational Safety and Best Practices
The intersection of high-power lasers (often Class 3B or Class 4, ranging from 500mW to 5W)
and generic DMX control introduces significant safety vectors that must be managed.

6.1 The "DMX Watchdog" Failure Mode
Many generic lasers (Big Dipper, Sumger) feature a "DMX Watchdog." If the DMX packet
stream is interrupted (cable unplugged, terminator missing), the firmware defaults to Sound
Active Mode.
●​ Scenario: A DMX cable fails during a quiet ballad. The laser loses signal, reverts to
Sound Active, picks up the kick drum from the monitors, and immediately starts scanning
full-power beams into the audience.
●​ Mitigation:
1.​ DMX Terminators: Mandatory to prevent signal reflection and packet loss.
2.​ Menu Settings: Navigate the fixture's menu to find the "No Signal" or "DMX Fail"
setting. Change it from "Sound" or "Auto" to "Blackout" or "Close." Many cheap
units lack this menu option; in such cases, the fixture must be powered via a
switched relay that can be killed remotely.

6.2 The "Static Beam" (Hot Beam) Hazard
In 34-channel mode, the "Size" channels (CH 7 and CH 24) control the scan angle.
●​ Risk: If a user accidentally brings the "Size" fader to 0, the X and Y galvos stop moving,
collapsing the entire energy of the laser (e.g., 2 Watts) into a single stationary point. This
can instantly cause retinal damage or ignite flammable materials.
●​ Mitigation:
○​ Profile Limits: In the console profile, set the Phys Min (Physical Minimum) of the
Zoom/Size channels to a DMX value of 10 or 20, preventing the user from ever
hitting 0.
○​ Invert Logic: Some fixtures (ShowNET standard) invert the zoom (0 = Full Size,
255 = Small). In this case, limit the Phys Max to 240.

6.3 Galvanometer Health and Scan Speeds
Generic lasers typically utilize galvos rated at 15kpps to 20kpps (kilo-points per second) at 8
degrees. High-end units run at 30kpps or 60kpps.
●​ The "Complex Pattern" Problem: Sending a highly complex ILDA pattern (like a text
sentence) via DMX CH 2 to a 15kpps generic laser will cause the scanners to struggle.
The image will distort, corners will round off, and the galvos will generate excessive heat,
leading to premature failure.
●​ Best Practice: When using generic lasers (U'King, Sumger), stick to low-vertex-count
patterns (beams, cones, fans, flat lines). Avoid complex graphics or text unless the unit
specifically advertises 30kpps+ scanners.

7. Troubleshooting and Diagnostic Guide
Symptom: The laser is responding to DMX (DMX LED is blinking), but no light is output.
●​ Diagnosis A: Channel 1 (Intensity/Mode) is not in the "Open" range. Try setting CH 1 to
255.
●​ Diagnosis B: The "Visible Points" or "Drawing" channel (CH 13 in 34ch mode) is set to 0.
Set it to 255.
●​ Diagnosis C: The interlock (remote safety connector) on the rear panel is missing or
loose. Generic lasers often ship with a "bypass plug" that must be inserted into the
Interlock port.
Symptom: The laser flickers erratically or changes patterns randomly.
●​ Diagnosis: DMX address conflict or "Mode" channel overlapping. The fixture might be
hovering on the boundary value between "Auto Mode" and "DMX Mode" (e.g., DMX value
191/192 on Big Dipper).
●​ Solution: Force the Mode channel deeper into the DMX range (e.g., 200+) and check
DMX addressing.
Symptom: Patterns A and B are clashing or creating a "messy" projection.
●​ Diagnosis: Both layers are active with complex patterns, overloading the
processor/galvos.
●​ Solution: Pull the fader for Channel 18 (Layer B Intensity) to 0. Use dual layers only for
simple geometry (e.g., Layer A = Circle, Layer B = Dot).

8. Conclusion and Future Outlook
The control of generic laser projection systems has matured from simple sound-active toys to
complex, vector-graphics engines capable of professional output. The key to unlocking this
potential lies in recognizing the ShowNET 34-channel standard as the unifying architecture
behind brands like Ehaho and Shehds, and distinguishing it from the legacy 6-channel U'King
and Big Dipper protocols.
For the systems integrator, the "generic" label is no longer a sign of incompatibility but rather an
indication of a specific, albeit undocumented, set of standards. By implementing the mapping
strategies detailed in this report—specifically the use of dual-head profiles for 34ch modes and
rigorous safety limits on Size and Mode channels—generic lasers can be seamlessly integrated
into high-level productions. As the market moves forward, the integration of APP control

(Bluetooth/WiFi) seen in Shehds units suggests a future where DMX might be bypassed entirely
for entry-level users, but for the professional, the 34-channel DMX protocol remains the
definitive bridge between budget hardware and world-class show control.
